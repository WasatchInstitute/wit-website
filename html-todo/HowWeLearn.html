HOW DO WE LEARN?


HOW WE LEARN

At WIT, we use Project based learning as an alternative to paper-based, rote memorization, or to teacher-led classrooms. Instead of having semesters or quarters we have sprints. A sprint is a three week period of time in which students focus on a certain theme in each of their classes. Each class molds the lessons that they teach to tie into that theme. Instead of having standardized tests at the end of each sprint we have deliverables. Students will have had deliverables that were due for each class before the sprint is over. They are given objectives during each sprint and the deliverables show that they have learned these objectives.


SPRINT 0 - BOATS

The first week at WIT, students had the unique opportunity to build boats out of cardboard. The only dilemma was that these boats had to float across a swimming pool with two passengers. We were given no directions and had to design, model, and build the boats all on our own. Once the boats were finished we took them to the Murray pool and had a competition to see whose boat could survive. To everyone’s surprise, many of the boats were able to float extremely well. This sprint was a “mini” sprint. It was only one week long, but it was a very fun and educational way to get the year started.


MUSIC SPRINT

During this sprint the theme was- you guessed it- music. The students got to learn about all kinds of aspects of music. In science, they got into groups and built wind chimes. Each group got chose a chord and then calculated the length necessary for each pipe to achieve the desired sound. In English, students wrote poems and participated in the Poetry Out Loud contest. Poetry Out Loud is a nation-wide competition in which students memorize and recite famous poems. In history, each student drew a composer and an instrument to write a report on. As a sort of SCOP the students who could play music wrote a piece and performed it. The rest of the students formed a choir. Additionally, there were some very cool field trips this sprint. For starters, the students went to the symphony, a violin making school, and the Mormon Tabernacle to look at their organ. This sprint was a big success with just about everybody involved.


WATER SPRINT

The water sprint was a memorable one. This sprint was jam-packed with field trips, and learning. A lot of students thought they knew a lot when it came to water, but once the sprint got started we realized we didn’t have the slightest clue. We spent three weeks going all over the place. We went to a water symposium, a water treatment plant, and a sewage plant. Each of the classes planned lessons that would open our eyes about water. In science we learned about watersheds and we got to do water testing in Little Cottonwood Canyon and Murray Pond, then write a lab report comparing our findings for the two different bodies of water. We tested the temperature, pH levels, dissolved oxygen levels, Abiotic factor, and turbidity. In math we created spreadsheets to determine how much water each of us, and our entire family used in one week. We then did an exercise where we grouped up into Families and added our personal totals together and had to tote our “family” water total for about a half mile. In computer science we started the process of writing an app that would calculate our family’s total water use. In English we wrote a paper to an elected official about an issue that we had learned about at the symposium we attended. This was one of the very popular sprints. In Social Studies, we talked about how people used to dispose of their water. We got into groups of three and we got assigned to cities that have major water problems and we did a report on them. This sprint was one of my favorites!
